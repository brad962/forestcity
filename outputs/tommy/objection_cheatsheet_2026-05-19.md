# Objection Handling Cheat Sheet
### Forest City Power Washing — Bradley's Sales Reference
*Tommy Greer | Content & Copy Writer*
*Generated: 2026-05-19*

---

Use this when a prospect pushes back after seeing an estimate. These responses work in person, over text, or on a call. The goal isn't to win an argument — it's to remove the one thing standing between them and booking.

---

## Objection 1: "It's too expensive."

**What they actually mean:** *"I'm not convinced the value justifies the cost right now."*

**Response:**
> "I get it. Can I ask — are you comparing us to another quote, or just the number itself?
>
> If it's the number: a full exterior wash typically runs $200–$400. Most customers tell me they've been putting it off for 2–3 years. That's $70–$130 a year. For a house that's protected from algae damage, mildew rot, and paint deterioration.
>
> If you don't clean it, the algae and mold keep working. Repainting a house runs $3,000–$8,000. Power washing is maintenance — it's actually the cheap option.
>
> Want me to break down what's in the quote so you can see exactly what you're getting?"

**Text version (for SMS follow-up):**
> "Totally fair question. Quick thought — what's been the biggest hesitation on the price? Happy to walk you through what goes into it. Most folks end up saving more down the road by catching it now."

---

## Objection 2: "I can do it myself."

**What they actually mean:** *"I own a pressure washer" or "How hard can it be?"*

**Response:**
> "You can for sure — and some people do. Here's the thing we run into though: most homeowners have a 1,500–2,500 PSI consumer washer. That's enough to clean a deck but won't touch roof algae without damaging the shingles. We use a soft wash system — lower pressure, biodegradable solution that actually kills the algae at the root instead of just moving it around.
>
> The algae on your roof and siding isn't just dirt — it's a living organism. If you don't kill it, it comes back in 60 days. Our wash typically holds 2–3 years.
>
> If you've got the equipment and time, go for it. I just want you to know what the difference is."

**Short version for a tight moment:**
> "Most consumer pressure washers don't have the PSI for siding or shingles. We soft wash — kills the algae at the root so it doesn't come back in a month. Makes the job last 2–3 years vs. a few weeks."

---

## Objection 3: "I'll wait until next year / after summer / when things slow down."

**What they actually mean:** *"Not a priority right now."*

**Response:**
> "That's totally fine. One thing to know: the algae doesn't stop growing while you wait. June through August is peak growth season in Northeast Ohio — between the lake humidity and the heat, that stuff doubles every 4–6 weeks.
>
> If you wait until fall, the job is bigger. If you wait until next year, there's a real chance you're looking at staining on the siding that won't come fully clean, or shingles that have started to absorb the algae deep into the material.
>
> I book out 3–4 weeks in peak season. If you want a May slot, I need to know this week. After that, I can't promise anything until June."

**Plant the urgency without pressure:**
> "No problem — just want to flag that May–June is when algae is most aggressive up here. Jobs that come in now are usually faster and cleaner than ones we see in August. Totally your call, just good to know."

---

## Objection 4: "We tried power washing before and it didn't last."

**What they actually mean:** *"I've been burned before and don't trust the result will hold."*

**Response:**
> "That makes sense, and it's a real problem in this industry. High-pressure washing doesn't kill the algae — it strips the surface and can damage siding and shingles if the operator isn't careful. Most of what you're seeing come back fast is because the root wasn't treated.
>
> We use a soft wash system — low pressure combined with a biodegradable solution that actually kills the algae, mold, and mildew at the source. The results hold 2–3 years on average, not 2–3 months.
>
> I'd honestly rather not do the job than have you call me in 90 days saying it came back. That's not what we're about."

**Closer:**
> "What company did the work before? I'm curious what approach they used. Either way, I'll give you a call in 3 months after we're done to make sure it's holding — that's something we do for every customer."

---

## Objection 5: "Let me talk to my wife/husband/partner."

**What they actually mean:** *"I'm interested but not ready to commit alone" — or sometimes — "I need an exit."*

**Response (text-friendly):**
> "Of course, no rush. I'll send you a quick summary of what the quote covers so you have it in writing — makes the conversation easier.
>
> One heads up: I'm booking 3–4 weeks out right now and Memorial Day week especially fills fast. If you know you want a May or early June date, it's worth giving me a heads-up before it's fully booked. Happy to hold a spot with no commitment while you talk it over."

**If it's been 48+ hours and no reply:**
> "Hey [name], just checking in. Did you get a chance to talk it over? No pressure either way — just want to make sure I have your spot held if you want it."

## Universal Closer (use at end of any conversation)

When the objection is handled and there's a pause:

> "So what do you want to do? I can get you on the schedule today and you don't have to think about it again."

Short. Direct. Works.
