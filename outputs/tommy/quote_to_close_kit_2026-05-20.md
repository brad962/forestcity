## THE SITUATION

Someone replied to your email. Maybe they said "interested," maybe they asked "how much?" either way — this is the moment. How you respond in the next 30 minutes determines whether this becomes a job or goes cold.

This kit covers the full chain: acknowledgment → discovery → site visit → quote → close.

## PART 2: DISCOVERY QUESTIONS
*Ask these before the site visit OR during a 5-minute phone call to scope the job.*

**For Property Managers:**
1. How many buildings does the property have, and roughly how many square feet of exterior surface?
2. What are the main problem areas — siding, roof, parking lot, sidewalks, or all of the above?
3. When was it last professionally cleaned? (helps set expectations on time/complexity)
4. Are you looking for one-time service or ongoing maintenance?
5. Is there a board/owner approval process, or can you move forward once you have a price?

**For Realtors:**
1. Is this pre-listing, or does the seller just need it done?
2. What's the timeline — when does it need to be done by?
3. Is it a house wash only, or does the driveway/roof need attention?
4. Is the property occupied? (affects scheduling and coordination)

**For Contractors / Referral Leads:**
1. What's the job — is it prep work before you start, or cleanup after?
2. What surface type — siding, concrete, wood deck, roof?
3. What's your timeline, and who's the homeowner's main contact?

**The most important question you'll ever ask:**
*"Once you have the quote, what does the decision-making process look like?"*
This one question tells you: is there a spouse to convince, a board to approve, a budget cycle to wait for? You'll know how hard to push and how fast they can move.

## PART 3: SITE VISIT CHECKLIST
*Bring your phone (camera + notepad app). This is a 20-30 minute walk.*

### Measure / Estimate
- [ ] Linear feet of house perimeter (walk the outside, rough count)
- [ ] Number of stories (1-story, 2-story, 3-story)
- [ ] Roof sq footage (approximate — roof area is typically 1.1–1.3x the footprint)
- [ ] Driveway dimensions (length x width in feet)
- [ ] Deck/patio square footage
- [ ] Number of parking spaces (for commercial)
- [ ] Number of buildings (for HOA / multi-unit)

### Assess Condition
- [ ] Algae/mildew level: light (surface only), moderate (deep-set), heavy (multi-year buildup)
- [ ] Stain type: green algae, black streaks (roof), rust stains, oil stains, salt/calcium
- [ ] Access issues: tight gates, low-clearance areas, landscaping in the way
- [ ] Safety concerns: steep roof pitch, fragile siding type (cedar shake, EIFS/stucco)

### Document
- [ ] Take 4-6 photos: front, back, problem areas, roof close-up
- [ ] Note any pre-existing damage (cracks, peeling paint, rotted wood) — protect yourself
- [ ] If commercial: note whether they have outdoor spigot access (water source for equipment)

## PART 4: PRICING REFERENCE
*Use these ranges when building a quote. Round to the nearest $25. Don't discount on the first quote — if they push back, add value before cutting price.*

| House wash — 2 story | $375–$575 | Standard NE Ohio colonial |

| Roof soft wash — ranch/1 story | $350–$500 | Light-moderate growth |

| Driveway cleaning | $125–$225 | Standard 2-car, concrete or asphalt |

### Combo Discounts (standard)
- House + Driveway: subtract $50 from combined price
- House + Roof: subtract $75 from combined price
- Full exterior (house + roof + driveway): subtract $100–$125 from combined price

### Commercial / HOA

| Service | Typical Range | Notes |
|---------|---------------|-------|
| Per building (residential-style) | $350–$700 | Based on stories + sq ft |
| Parking lot striping prep wash | $200–$500 | Per lot section |
| Sidewalks / walkways (per 1,000 sq ft) | $100–$175 | Commercial rate |
| Dumpster pad / loading dock | $150–$350 | Grease/waste buildup premium |
| Fleet exterior wash (per vehicle) | $40–$75 | On-site; volume discount at 10+ |

**Minimum job: $175 for any residential, $250 for any commercial.**

**NE Ohio market note:** You are priced slightly above budget operators and below premium competitors. If someone tells you they got a quote for $150 on a 2-story house, they're being told they'll get pressure washing, not soft wash. Explain the difference — soft wash is safer and lasts 3x longer.

## PART 6: HANDLING "YOUR PRICE IS TOO HIGH"

This happens. Here's how to respond — don't apologize, don't discount immediately.

**Step 1 — acknowledge and ask:**
> "I hear you. Can I ask — what were you expecting, or did you get a number from someone else?"

**If they got a competitor quote:**
> "Most quotes that come in lower are for pressure washing, which can damage siding and roof shingles. What we do is soft wash — it's a chemical treatment that kills the algae at the root instead of just blasting the surface. It lasts 3 years instead of 6 months. The higher price reflects that."

**If they just didn't expect this price range:**
> "Totally fair — let me see what I can do. If you combine the house wash with the driveway, I can bring the total from $[price] down to $[discounted combo price]. That's usually the best value."

**If they're still pushing back:**
> "I'd rather lose a job than cut corners. I've had to re-clean properties that were damaged by high-pressure companies — it's not cheap to fix. But I understand if the budget's tight. No hard feelings."

This positioning — fair price, explains the difference, doesn't cave immediately — wins more jobs than discounting ever does.
